Thomas Edison was an inventor. His workshop in Menlo Park NJ, now Edison NJ, gave birth to a multitude of the inventions we in the 21st century take for granted; most influential of which is the incandescent lightbulb.
[S5] Born on February 11, 1847, Thomas Edison would become the single most influential inventor of his time. By 1882 he had ushered in the age of electricity by creating the first commercial power station to sell electricity and lights to those living nearby in Manhattan, creating the market for his products. Edison's electric companies would later come together to become Edison's General Electric and after more mergers, just General Electric. Edison would die in 1931 at the age of 84.
When information was first being shared to the public about the lightbulb there was a decent amount of convincing that still had to be done. [S1] Newspapers were used to flaunt the cheap price, that it would “cost not more than 25 cents.” and be "cheaper then oil" [S4] in comparison to the gas lamps of the day and explain the how the bulb functions. [S3] Edison would file a patent for his first major lamp breakthrough lamp in 1878 for a platinum filament lamp.[O2] This lamp was successful but did not catch on due to its energy inefficiency, low amount of light it produced and unnatural cool tone. This first proof of concept comes merely a year before his carbon filament lamp, having said “All the problems which have been puzzling me for the last 18 months have been solved” [S1] for a newspaper article.
[S1] The power efficiency of the carbon bulb was heavily stressed, pushing to the potential customers that both the bulbs and wiring were cheap. The price of the carbon bulb compared to the platinum bulb, and especially gas lamps was much lower. With this lower price of the bulb, the price to run electric cables was also significantly cheaper then the price of pipes, and since the cables used were the same as telegraph wire, people would have been more familiar with the price and ease of running.
